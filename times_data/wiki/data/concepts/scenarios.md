TIMES is designed around a scenario-based approach to exploring possible energy futures. A scenario is a coherent set of assumptions about future trajectories of key drivers.

## Four Components of a Scenario

Every TIMES scenario consists of:

1. **Demand component:** Energy service demand projections, driven by GDP, population, households, and sectoral output. Demands can be elastic (responding to prices) or fixed.

2. **Supply component:** Primary resource supply curves with multi-step potentials and costs. Includes renewable potentials, fossil fuel reserves, and trade possibilities.

3. **Policy component:** Regulations, taxes, subsidies, emission caps, portfolio standards, and other policy instruments. Represented through bounds, taxes, and user constraints.

4. **Techno-economic component:** The technology database describing all current and future technologies with their costs, efficiencies, lifetimes, and other characteristics.

## Reference vs. Policy Scenarios

- **Reference scenario (BAU):** Represents business-as-usual with no new policies
- **Policy scenarios:** Apply specific policy instruments (carbon tax, emission caps, etc.) and measure their impact compared to the reference

## Robustness Analysis

TIMES offers several tools for testing scenario robustness:
- **Sensitivity analysis** (Chapter 6): Systematic parameter variation
- **Stochastic programming** (Chapter 8): Multiple scenarios with probabilities
- **Limited foresight** (Chapter 9): Myopic decision-making
